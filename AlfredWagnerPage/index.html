Alfred Lothar Wegener

The man who believed continents moved

Here's a time line of Dr. Wegener's life:

  • November 1, 1880 - Born Berlin, Germany
  • 1905 - Awarded a PhD in astronomy from the University of Berlin.
  • 1906-08 - Took part in a paleoclimatology expedition to Greenland to study polar air circulation.
  • 1912 to 1930 - Made three more expeditions to Greenland.
  • 1915 - Published his work Die Entstehung der Kontinente und Ozeane(The Origin of Continents and Oceans)
  • 1924 to 1930 - Professor of meteorology and geophysics at the University of Graz.
  • 1930 - Died in Greenland while on his third expedition.

Wegener observed the similarity of the coastlines of eastern South America and Western Africa and speculated that those lands had once been joined. His research led him to a hypothesis positing about 250 million years ago all present day continents were formed as a single large continent, or supercontinent, he named Pangaea. Wegener’s hypothesis became known as continental drift. He proposed the world’s continents had slowly moved thousands of miles apart over very long periods of time. In his work The Origin of Continents and Oceans, he was able to point to many closely related fossils and rock strata found to occur on widely separated continents, including most importantly, North and South America, and Africa. Wegener garnered some adherents in the years following up to his death. After his death in1930 his theory, having been rejected by most geologists, sank into obscurity. Wegener’s theory was given credence in the 1960’s with the theory of plate tectonics.
